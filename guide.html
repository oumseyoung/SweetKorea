Guide to Korean Desserts

Popular Korean Desserts You Must Try

Korea’s dessert culture is vibrant, rich, and reflective of its deep history and innovative spirit. Here is a brief guide to some iconic desserts you shouldn’t miss.

Yakgwa

Yakgwa is a type of yumilgwa—a traditional Korean sweet made by mixing wheat flour and honey into a dough and then deep-frying it in oil. Historically, yakgwa is considered Korea’s most luxurious and extravagant confectionery. It was widely enjoyed throughout Korean history, particularly by royalty, aristocrats, temples, and common households. During the Goryeo dynasty, yakgwa was so extensively consumed that historical records mention: “The royal family, aristocrats, and temples excessively produce yumilgwa (oil-and-honey sweets), causing significant waste of grain, honey, and oil, leading to rising prices and hardship for ordinary people.” This indicates the luxurious nature and societal impact of yakgwa at the time. The name Yakgwa translates to “medicinal confection,” referring to its original meaning as a “medicinal sweet.” According to the works Ojuyeonmunjangjeonsango and Jibongyuseol, the ingredients, especially wheat, are said to undergo growth and maturation through all four seasons, symbolizing harmony with nature’s cycles. The 1948 publication Joseon Sangsik praised yakgwa highly: “Among all the sweets produced in Joseon, yakgwa is the finest and most distinctive treat, unmatched globally due to the immense care and devotion required in its preparation.” It is further described as uniquely exquisite due to the meticulous craftsmanship involved. Originally, yakgwa means “medicinal confectionery,” reflecting the belief in its beneficial properties. Its shape has evolved—initially mimicking various fruits or birds, eventually becoming round, and then square for practicality in stacking during ancestral rites. Yi Ik’s Seongho Saseol mentions this shift to square-shaped yakgwa, suggesting practical reasons related to ritual ceremonies. Yakgwa is prepared by mixing wheat flour and honey into dough and then deep-frying it thoroughly in oil. The slow frying process results in multiple flaky layers within the pastry. The layers absorb honey and oil deeply, giving yakgwa its distinctly rich, sweet, and aromatic flavor.

Jeonggwa

Jeonggwa is a traditional Korean confectionery made by simmering relatively dry ingredients, such as roots, stems, or fruits that are easily available around us, in sugar syrup and grain syrup for a long time until they become chewy and sweet. Yakgwa, a type of Jeonggwa, was traditionally prepared and consumed widely during the first lunar month and was considered essential for various rites of passage, traditional holidays, festive gatherings, and ancestral rites. Yakgwa was already widely popular during the Goryeo Dynasty and enjoyed extensively among royalty, nobility, Buddhist temples, and common households. Jeonggwa, meanwhile, is documented in Jeong Yak-yong’s Aheongakbi as being “boiled down in honey,” and thus was commonly known as a confection simmered in honey. In 1815, the Gyuhap Chongseo describes methods for making Jeonggwa by boiling ingredients in honey or preserving them in honey for later use. Traditionally, honey was mostly used for simmering Jeonggwa, but in 1854, the cookbook Eumsikbeop introduced a recipe for candied citrus (gamja-jeonggwa) simmered in sugar. Cookbooks detail up to 34 different types of Jeonggwa, with candied lotus root (yeongeun-jeonggwa) being the most frequently mentioned. A detailed recipe for lotus root Jeonggwa appeared in the 1958 Korean cookbook, Eumsik Mandeuneun Beop, describing: “Wash lotus roots thoroughly, slice into coins roughly one pun thick, boil in plenty of water until soft, drain the water, add three hops of honey along with sugar, then simmer over charcoal fire for three hours until suitably cooked, exhibiting a dark-reddish color, glossy surface, and chewy texture.” Doraji Jeonggwa (candied bellflower root) follows a similar cooking method. The 1715 book Sanlim Gyeongje introduced it as “Gilgyeong-ja,” describing how bellflower roots were peeled, their bitterness reduced by soaking, simmered in honey, or preserved in honey, dried in sunlight, and then stored for later use. Candied ginger (Saenggang-jeonggwa) appears in the 1913 cookbook, Joseon Yori Jebeop: “Peel ginger, slice thinly, boil in plenty of water to remove spiciness, discard water, boil again in fresh water, discard again, then add 3 hops of water with sugar and simmer until the quantity of liquid matches the ginger pieces, then remove.” Sansuyu-jeonggwa (candied cornelian cherry) is prepared by thoroughly washing and removing seeds, partially drying on a tray, and then preserving in honey. Making maesil-jeonggwa (candied plum) involves washing plums, drying excess water, preserving them in honey inside a jar, and then drying them again on a tray before use. Yuzu- jeonggwa (candied citron peel) is prepared by peeling and thinly slicing yuzu skin, briefly boiling it, and draining it to maintain its characteristic aroma. Well-made Yuzu-jeonggwa is notable for its subtle fragrance and rich nutritional value.

Ddungcaron

If you love classic French macarons, you’ll be amazed by Ddungcaron—Korea’s bold and creative take on this delicate dessert. The name Ddungcaron comes from the Korean word “뚱뚱하다” (ddungddunghada), meaning “chubby” or “fat,” and “macaron.” True to its name, Ddungcarons are extra thick and generously filled, making them a visually striking and highly indulgent treat. Traditional French macarons are known for their delicate, crisp shells and smooth fillings, usually consisting of ganache, buttercream, or jam. In contrast, Ddungcarons are significantly thicker, with an exaggerated amount of filling that can be nearly twice the height of the shells. The fillings go beyond the classic flavors and often include ingredients such as: • Cheesecake & Cream Cheese – Rich, tangy fillings that complement the sweetness of the shell. • Fresh Fruits & Jams – Strawberry, mango, or raspberry jams for a fresh and tangy contrast. • Cereal & Crunchy Toppings – Bits of cornflakes, cookie crumbs, or chocolate chips for added texture. • Korean Flavors – Green tea, black sesame, injeolmi (roasted soybean powder), and red bean. Some Ddungcarons are even stuffed with whole pieces of chocolate bars, fruit slices, or even mini cakes to make them more exciting. These over-the-top creations make Ddungcarons a popular choice for food bloggers and dessert lovers seeking the next trendy treat. The Ddungcaron trend gained momentum in Korea during the late 2010s as dessert cafés and patisseries began competing to create the most unique and Instagram-worthy versions of this indulgent treat. A key factor in its popularity was its strong social media appeal. With their bold colors, oversized fillings, and creative designs, Ddungcarons quickly became a highly photogenic dessert, making them perfect for platforms like Instagram and TikTok. Many cafés took advantage of this by designing Ddungcarons with cute character faces, seasonal decorations, and innovative flavor combinations, attracting both food lovers and influencers. Beyond social media, Korea’s vibrant dessert café culture also played a significant role in the rise of Ddungcarons. Koreans have a deep appreciation for dessert cafés, where aesthetic presentation and unique flavors are highly valued. Ddungcarons fit seamlessly into this culture, offering a visually striking and delicious treat that pairs well with coffee, milk tea, or traditional Korean teas like yuzu or green tea. Another factor contributing to their widespread appeal is the availability of personalized and seasonal variations. Many bakeries introduce limited-edition flavors for special occasions such as Christmas, Valentine’s Day, and Halloween, keeping customers excited for new releases. Additionally, Ddungcarons can be customized with personalized messages, making them a popular gift choice. This combination of visual appeal, café culture integration, and customization options has solidified Ddungcarons as a must-try dessert in Korea.
